it prints every phone line your machine has open right now, who's listening on each, and who (if anyone) is currently connected to it.

  When you run docker compose up, Postgres opens phone line 5432 and Redis opens 6379. netstat lets you walk up to the switchboard and confirm:
  "Yes, those two lines are live, and here's exactly how they're configured."

  The command, decoded

  netstat -ano | findstr "5432 6379"
  - netstat — dump the switchboard log
  - -a — all sockets (listening ones included, not just active connections)
  - -n — numeric: show 5432, don't translate it to the friendly name "postgresql". Faster and unambiguous.
  - -o — show the owning process ID (PID)
  - | findstr "5432 6379" — filter the huge list down to just lines mentioning our two ports

  Reading the output

  You'll get something like this (5 columns):

    Proto  Local Address      Foreign Address   State       PID
    TCP    127.0.0.1:5432     0.0.0.0:0         LISTENING   12345
    TCP    127.0.0.1:6379     0.0.0.0:0         LISTENING   12345

  So the three big takeaways an engineer pulls from it



  Idea 1: "the whole network"

  Imagine your laptop is a house.

  Your house has two kinds of doorbells:
  - 🔔 An inside doorbell — only people already inside your house can ring it.
  - 🔔 A front-door doorbell — anyone outside (your phone, a neighbor, anyone on the same wifi) can ring it.

  Postgres (your database) is a person sitting inside the house who answers doorbells.

  You told Postgres: "only answer the inside doorbell."

  So only programs on your own laptop can reach the database. Nobody else on the wifi can. That's it. "The whole network" is just a fancy way
  of saying "everyone else connected to the same wifi as you." You chose to keep them out. Good.

  That's what 127.0.0.1 means: inside doorbell only.

  Idea 2: PID

  Every program running on your computer gets a number, like a name tag at a party.

  That number is called the PID.

  It's just so the computer can tell programs apart. "Program wearing tag #15472." Nothing deeper than that.

  When you ran netstat, it told you "the program holding port 5432 is wearing tag #15472." That program happens to be Docker.

  ---

  "The whole network"

  Your laptop can be reached at more than one address at the same time:

  - 127.0.0.1 — called localhost or loopback. Think of it as an internal intercom inside your house. A call to it never leaves the building.
  Only programs running on your own laptop can reach it.
  - Your wifi address — something like 192.168.1.42. This is your laptop's front-door address on the network you're connected to. Your phone, a
  roommate's laptop, anything else on the same wifi can dial it.

  Now the key part. 0.0.0.0 is a wildcard meaning "listen on ALL my addresses at once" — the intercom and the front door.

  ports: "5432:5432"            → binds 0.0.0.0  → DB answers the front door too
                                   → anyone on your wifi can try to reach your database

  ports: "127.0.0.1:5432:5432"  → binds 127.0.0.1 → DB only answers the internal intercom
                                   → only YOUR laptop can reach it   ✅ what you chose

  That's "the whole network" — every other device sharing your wifi/router. You deliberately kept Postgres on the intercom only. Your netstat
  shows 127.0.0.1, so it's locked down correctly.

  1. Reachability — LISTENING means it's actually serving (beyond "the container started").
  2. Security posture — 127.0.0.1 vs 0.0.0.0 tells you instantly whether you accidentally exposed a service to the network.
  3. Ownership & conflicts — the PID is your weapon for diagnosing "port already in use."
