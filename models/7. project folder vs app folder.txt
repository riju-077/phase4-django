 Project vs App

  dashboard/ = the project. It's the hotel building itself.
  - The front desk, the wiring, the plumbing, the address.
  - settings.py, root urls.py, wsgi.py/asgi.py — building-wide config.
  - There is exactly one project per Django site.

  weather/ = an app. It's one department/floor of the hotel.
  - The "weather department" — its own models, views, templates, logic.
  - Self-contained: in theory you could lift the weather app out and drop it into another hotel.

  dashboard/   ← the hotel (project)   — one only
  └── weather/ ← a department (app)    — many allowed
      └── (later maybe: accounts/, billing/, ...)

  Your actual question: one app or many?

  You're exactly right — it depends on the project. The rule of thumb:

  ▎ One app = one cohesive area of functionality.

  In Phase 1–3 you happened to have one project + one app, so it looked like a fixed "two folders" rule. It's not. A real project can have many
  apps:

  dashboard/        (project)
  ├── weather/      (weather data, charts)
  ├── accounts/     (login, OTP, profiles)
  └── billing/      (payments)

  Big companies split aggressively. We could split OTP into its own accounts app — but for Phase 4 we'll keep OTP inside weather to stay
  focused. Fewer apps = simpler; more apps = better separation when things grow.

  The mental shortcut

  - Project = settings + URLs + the glue. One.
  - App = a feature with its own models/views. As many as the project needs.

● Here's the story. 🏨 

  The Grand Hotel

  You're hired to build the Grand Dashboard Hotel. On day one, you don't decorate rooms — you build the building.

  So you pour the foundation, run the electrical wiring, lay the plumbing, put a sign with the address out front, and hire a front desk that
  greets every visitor and points them to the right floor.

  That's dashboard/ — the project. The building. There's only ever one of it, because a hotel has one address, one front desk, one main fuse
  box (settings.py), and one lobby directory that says "Weather → Floor 3" (urls.py).

  ---
  Now — an empty building is useless. Guests don't come to admire the plumbing. They come for services. So you start opening departments.

  You open the Weather Department. It gets its own floor, its own staff, its own filing cabinets full of temperature records, its own service
  desk. The hotel's front desk just routes guests to it — but everything weather-related happens inside that department.

  That's weather/ — an app. A self-contained department with its own filing cabinets (models.py), its own staff who do the work (views.py), its
  own reception layout (templates/).

  ---
  Now the magic moment that makes it click:

  A rival hotel across town says "we love your Weather Department — can we have one too?" Because the department is self-contained, you
  literally pack up the whole weather/ folder, ship it over, and plug it into their building. It works, because it never depended on your
  specific hotel — only on being plugged into some hotel's front desk.

  That's the whole point of an app: a reusable department you can move between buildings.

  ---
  Why "two folders" wasn't a rule

  In Phase 1–3 your hotels each had one department, so you saw "one building + one department" and assumed that was the law. But a real hotel
  grows:

  🏨 dashboard/   ← the building (ONE)
     ├── 🌡️  weather/    ← Weather Department
     ├── 🔐 accounts/   ← Guest Check-in / ID verification
     └── 💳 billing/    ← Payments Department

  A tiny B&B has one department. A resort has twenty. The building is always one; the departments are however many the business needs.

  ---
  So the rule in one line:

  ▎ Project = the building (one). App = a department (as many as the project needs).
