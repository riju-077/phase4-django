Now your question — and it's a really good one, this is exactly the thing to be curious about:

▎ Why check the DB when we're fetching from someone else's API?

Here's the key idea, and it's the heart of this whole project's design:

Your dashboard page never talks to the weather API. Ever.

Two totally separate jobs happen at different times:

Job 1 — Fetching (rare, slow, background):
The Open-Meteo API is far away on the internet. Calling it takes a second or two, and sometimes it's down. So we only call it occasionally — in the background — and every time we do, we copy the answer into our own database.

Job 2 — Showing the page (constant, fast, instant):
When you (or 100 users) load the dashboard, we read from our own database, which is sitting right next to us. Instant. Always available.

---
Think of it like a water tank on your roof:

- The API is the city water main — far away, you don't want every tap in your house calling the water company directly.
- The background fetch fills your rooftop tank now and then.
- Every tap in the house (every page load) draws from the tank, not the water main.

Your DB is the tank. The dashboard drinks from the tank.

Why this matters:
- ⚡ Fast — reading your own DB beats an internet round-trip every time.
- 🛡️ Reliable — if Open-Meteo goes down, your page still works (tank still has water).
- 🚦 Polite — you're not hammering a free API on every single page view.

That's why "is there data in the DB?" was the right question — the page's whole world is the DB.
