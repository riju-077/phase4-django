But a view alone does nothing

A view is just a function sitting in a file. Nothing calls it yet. For it to run, it needs a web address pointing at it — like /fetch/. Right now there's no such address.

Step 2 of 3: give it a URL

This is the part where you tell Django "when someone visits /fetch/, run trigger_fetch." It's the same URL wiring you did in Phases 1–2 — nothing new.

You need two small pieces:
1. A new file weather/urls.py (your app doesn't have one yet)
2. One line in dashboard/urls.py to plug the weather app's URLs in



Both files written. Here's how they chain together to make the address /fetch/ work:

Someone visits  /fetch/
        │
dashboard/urls.py  →  path("", include("weather.urls"))
        │              "for URLs, go look in the weather app"
        ▼
weather/urls.py    →  path("fetch/", views.trigger_fetch)
        │              "/fetch/ ? run trigger_fetch"
        ▼
weather/views.py   →  trigger_fetch runs → .delay() drops the job in Redis 📦

The include(...) line is like a receptionist saying "weather questions? down the hall, weather app handles those." That's why big Django projects give each app its own urls.py instead of cramming everything into one file.

Step 3 of 3: run it and watch the job fire 🎬

This is the payoff. You need two terminals open at the same time, because two separate programs must run together (remember: the webpage and the worker are different programs).

Terminal 1 — the website:
python manage.py runserver

Terminal 2 — the worker (the "delivery guy" watching Redis):
celery -A dashboard worker --loglevel=info --pool=solo

▎ --pool=solo matters on Windows — Celery's normal mode misbehaves on Windows, and solo runs it in a simple single-process way that just works. Leave it in.

Then: open your browser to

- Terminal 2 (the worker) → a few lines appear: Received task: weather.tasks.fetch_weather … then succeeded. That is the moment Celery clicks — the delivery guy just picked up your order and ran it.
