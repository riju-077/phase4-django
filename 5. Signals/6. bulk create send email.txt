Let me connect it directly.

The line:
EMAIL_BACKEND = 'django.core.mail.backends.console.EmailBackend'

The word that matters is console.

In Django, a "backend" just means "which method do I use to do this job?" For email, Django offers a few different methods:

┌───────────────────────────┬────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│    Backend you choose     │          What "send email" actually does           │
├───────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ smtp (the real one)       │ Connects to Gmail → email lands in a real inbox 📧 │
├───────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ console (what you picked) │ Prints the email as text in the terminal 🖥️        │
└───────────────────────────┴────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

You picked console. So every time any code calls send_mail(...), Django looks at that setting, sees the word console, and goes: "Ah, my instructions say print it to the terminal — so that's what I'll do."

It's like a mailbox setting on your phone:
- Set it to "Gmail" → messages go out to the world.
- Set it to "Drafts only" → messages just show up on your own screen, nothing leaves.

You flipped the switch to "show it on my own screen."

So the chain is:

Your signal calls send_mail(...)
   ↓
Django checks EMAIL_BACKEND in settings
   ↓
sees "console"
   ↓
prints the email in your runserver terminal  ✅

Change that one line to the real Gmail backend later, and the exact same send_mail code would send a real email instead — you wouldn't touch the signal at all. That's the whole point of it being a setting: swap the method, keep the code.
