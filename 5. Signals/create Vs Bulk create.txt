🏨 The Grand Hotel.

You run a hotel. You have a rule:

▎ "Every time a guest checks in, the concierge must run over and hand them a welcome gift."

The concierge = your signal. The "guest checks in" event = saving a row. The concierge only moves when he hears the check-in bell ring. 🔔

---
Monday — one guest arrives.

A single guest walks to the front desk. The receptionist checks him in properly: takes his name, gives him a key, and dings the bell. 🔔

The concierge hears it, runs over, hands him the welcome gift. 🎁

▎ This is OTPCode.objects.create(...) — your working OTP signal. One guest, bell rings, concierge reacts. It worked.

---
Tuesday — a tour bus of 24 people arrives.

The receptionist looks at the huge line and thinks: "If I check in all 24 one by one and ding the bell 24 times, it'll take an hour. Let me be fast."

So instead, he grabs all 24 passports at once, stamps them in one big batch, hands out 24 keys in one sweep — and walks them in through the side door.

It's lightning fast. ⚡ All 24 are now in their rooms.

But he never rang the bell. Not once.

So the concierge is still sitting at his post, reading the newspaper. He has no idea 24 guests just arrived. Nobody got a welcome gift. 🎁❌

▎ This is WeatherReading.objects.bulk_create([...24 rows...]). Fast, because it skips the bell. Your alert signal (the concierge) never finds out, never sends the email.

---
The punchline:

The receptionist isn't broken. The concierge isn't broken. The problem is the fast batch path skips the bell on purpose — that's why it's fast.

So later, when our weather fetch uses the tour-bus method (bulk_create) and you expect a temperature-alert email... the concierge never heard the bell. No email. That's the trap I want you to remember.

One line to keep:

▎ 🔔 create() rings the bell (signal fires). 🔕 bulk_create() uses the side door (signal does not fire).
